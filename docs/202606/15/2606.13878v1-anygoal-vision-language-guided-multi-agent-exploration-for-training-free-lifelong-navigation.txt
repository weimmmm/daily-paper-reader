# AnyGoal: Vision-Language Guided Multi-Agent Exploration for Training-Free Lifelong Navigation 

MoniJesu James Marcelino Julio Fernando Miguel Altamirano Cabrera Dzmitry Tsetserukou 

Abstract: End-to-end navigation policies trained on large simulation corpora degrade sharply when transferred to scenes, target categories, or goal modalities outside their training distribution. The leading zero-shot alternatives inherit a dif-ferent but equally constraining ceiling: modular pipelines such as Modular GOAT are bottlenecked by the recall of a closed-set object detector, while recent 3D snapshot–memory systems (e.g. 3D-Mem) accumulate dense, view-dependent rep-resentations that are heavy to maintain and difficult to share across embodiments. We present AnyGoal , a fully decentralized, training-free multi-robot architecture that places a Vision–Language Model (VLM) at the cognitive core of frontier-based exploration and coordinates the swarm through a shared 2D Gaussian Bayesian Value Map (BVM). The BVM maintains a per-pixel (μ, σ 2) posterior over goal rel-evance, updated by precision-weighted fusion of VLM scores through a depth-cone mask, and is never reset between subtasks , yielding lifelong evidence accumula-tion. Frontiers are ranked by a convex blend of a VLM-as-judge softmax and a Bayesian Upper-Confidence-Bound (UCB) term computed directly on the BVM. A sequential greedy allocator with a spatial-separation penalty and commitment hysteresis distributes frontiers across agents without any centralized controller. On the full GOAT-B ENCH val unseen split (360 episodes, 2,669 subtasks), our dual-agent system achieves 52.4 % Subtask SR at 12.7 % SPL, state-of-the-art under the strict physical regime (discrete 0.25 m steps, no teleportation, 42 ◦ HFOV) and a +27.5 percentage-point improvement over the published Modular GOAT baseline (24.9 %). The single-agent AnyGoal configuration alone achieves 41.9 % 

Subtask SR, demonstrating that the gains arise from the underlying decision archi-tecture rather than from raw agent count. A controlled four-way perception ablation shows that high-recall open-vocabulary detectors shift the dominant failure mode of zero-shot navigation away from exploration and onto goal verification at the stopping decision .

Keywords: Multi-Modal Navigation, Vision-Language Models, Multi-Agent Co-ordination, Bayesian Mapping, Zero-Shot Embodied AI 

## 1 Introduction 

Embodied agents that operate in real homes will be asked, in a single deployment, to find an object by name, by photograph, and by free-form natural language description, all while persisting useful memory across tasks. GOAT-B ENCH [1 ] crystallized this requirement as multi-modal lifelong navigation : an agent receives a stream of 5–10 heterogeneous subtasks per episode in photorealistic HM3D scenes [ 2 ], with no scene or category supervision at training time. Two regimes have emerged on this benchmark. End-to-end policies, most prominently SenseAct-NN Skill Chain [ 1] and the concurrent AstraNav-Memory [ 3], attain strong path efficiency on familiar distributions, but their reliance on learned spatial priors makes them brittle under category, modality, and embodiment shift. Modular zero-shot pipelines such as Modular GOAT [ 1 ] side-step training by composing off-the-shelf detection, mapping, and planning stacks, but inherit the closed-vocabulary ceiling of detectors such as DETIC [ 4]; on GOAT-B ENCH val unseen the strongest Modular GOAT variant attains 24 .9 % Subtask SR and 17 .2 % SPL. A more recent line of work, exemplified by 3D-Mem [ 5],

replaces the implicit detector with a view-dependent 3D snapshot database queried by a VLM. This restores open-vocabulary recall at the cost of substantial memory and compute overhead, and remains structurally single-agent. We argue that neither extreme is necessary. The spatial reasoning that modular systems delegate to fragile detectors and the semantic reasoning that 3D systems delegate to per-snapshot VLM calls can be unified inside a single lightweight 2D structure, a Gaussian Bayesian Value Map , if the VLM is treated as a noisy oracle whose answers are fused into a persistent spatial posterior. Once frontiers carry calibrated (μ, σ 2) belief, they admit a principled blend of exploitation (VLM relevance) and 

exploration (UCB on the BVM), and they become a natural medium for decentralized multi-agent allocation: each agent reads the same map but acts greedily under a separation penalty that no centralized scheduler has to enforce. 

Contributions. This paper introduces AnyGoal , a fully decentralized, training-free multi-robot architecture for multi-modal lifelong navigation, and validates it through the most comprehensive perception ablation reported on GOAT-B ENCH val unseen to date. Concretely: 1. A Gaussian Bayesian Value Map for lifelong relevance. We replace the Beta-Bernoulli and binary occupancy heuristics common in zero-shot navigation [ 6, 7 ] with a precision-weighted Gaussian update over per-pixel (μ, σ 2) goal-relevance belief, fused through a depth-cone observa-tion mask. The map is never reset between subtasks, supporting cross-subtask evidence carry-over and natural hallucination decay. 2. Convex-blended VLM-as-judge with Bayesian UCB. Candidate frontiers are rendered as A/B/C/D markers on the egocentric view; the VLM returns a softmax. We blend the softmax with a UCB term on the BVM via a convex coefficient ( w = 0 .50 ), gated by an explicit “is-worth” Yes/No prompt ( ≥ 0.50 ). This recovers the ergodic exploration guarantees of UCB while letting the VLM dominate when its preference is sharp. 3. Decentralized swarm coordination without explicit messaging. Frontier allocation is a se-quential greedy bid over the shared BVM with a spatial-separation penalty ( d < 100 px ⇒ × 0.6)and a 1.5× commitment hysteresis. No centralized controller and no explicit inter-agent message channel are required beyond the shared map. 4. Headline results on GOAT-B ENCH . On the full val unseen split (360 episodes, 2,669 subtasks), AnyGoal with N = 2 ground agents achieves 52.42 % subtask SR and 12.66 % SPL, exceeding Modular GOAT by +27.5 pp and the strongest zero-shot single-agent system TANGO by +20.3 pp 

on subtask SR. Our N = 1 baseline already attains 42.0 % subtask SR, demonstrating that the architectural prior, not raw agent count, is doing most of the work. 5. A scientific characterization of the perception bottleneck. A four-way ablation across SAM3, SAM3+DINOv2, GroundedSAM, and YOLO-World+MobileSAM, with detector quality varied and every other component held fixed, shows that the dominant failure mode of zero-shot navi-gation shifts as recall increases: high-recall SAM3 fails through premature STOP calls ( 80 .2 % 

of failures), while low-recall YOLO-World exhausts its step budget ( 42 .3 % ). This reframes the open problem from “find better detectors” to “verify the goal at the stopping decision”. The remainder of the paper develops these claims. Section 2 positions AnyGoal against the closed-set modular line, the 3D snapshot-memory line, and centralized multi-agent reinforcement learning; Section 3 details the BVM, the convex-blended UCB score, and the decentralized allocator; Section 4 reports the evaluation protocol; Sections 5–6 present the state-of-the-art result, the multi-agent scaling curve, the four-way perception ablation, and a discussion that interprets SPL across simulation regimes; Section 7 states the remaining limitations.

Memory in embodied AI. Navigation memory has moved from semantic occupancy grids [ 11 ]to 3D snapshot representations. 3D-Mem [ 5] is the strongest recent exponent: a per-snapshot 

3D-fragment database re-queried by a VLM at every decision point ( 49 .6 % Episode SR, 28 .8 % 

SPL). The representation is single-agent and view-dependent, and the cost of re-rendering snapshots through a VLM grows linearly in episode length and quadratically with agent count. The BVM takes the opposite stance, a 2D per-pixel Gaussian posterior whose storage is constant in episode length and whose merge across agents is a closed-form precision-weighted sum, placing AnyGoal in the direct lineage of value-map navigation [ 6 , 12 ] but with uncertainty as a first-class quantity that drives both UCB exploration and the rejection of spatially inconsistent detections. VLFM [ 6]is the conceptual ancestor of this line but reports only on single-modality O BJECT NAV and has no published GOAT-B ENCH val unseen number; we therefore do not include it as a direct baseline and instead position the Gaussian BVM as its principled multi-modal, uncertainty-aware extension. 

Zero-shot and multi-agent robotics. Zero-shot semantic navigation has progressed via VLM-grounded frontier methods [ 6, 7 , 12 ], LLM-planned skill chains [ 13 , 12 ], graph captioning pipelines such as TANGO [ 14 ], and unified-goal frameworks such as UniGoal [ 15 ]; trained counterparts include MTU3D [ 16 ]. All are overwhelmingly single-agent. Multi-agent navigation is dominated by centralized MARL [ 17 , 18 ] that requires training, reward shaping, and tight communication assumptions. We instead reduce coordination to a decentralized cost-utility bid over the shared BVM, in spirit closest to classical decentralized auctions [ 19 , 20 ] but operating over an uncertainty-aware value map rather than a flat information-gain field, with no explicit messaging beyond map synchronization. 

## 3 Method 

3.1 System architecture 

AnyGoal is structured as four swappable layers (Fig. 1). At each decision step, every agent i runs the same local pipeline: 31. Perception. An open-vocabulary segmentation backbone (SAM3 [ 10 ] by default; Grounded-SAM [ 21 ] and YOLO-World [ 9] as ablations) emits goal-conditioned masks; a spatial VLM (SpaceOM-3B) provides relevance scores; and for image-goal subtasks an image matcher (CLIP ViT-B/32 [22] by default; DINOv2 [23] as ablation) supplies similarity to the reference image. 2. Mapping. Detections are back-projected through depth into a shared BEV occupancy grid at 

0.05 m /cell by a GoalProjector that rejects spatially inconsistent VLM hits; the Bayesian Value Map (§3.2) maintains goal-relevance belief on the same grid. 3. Reasoning. A four-stage prompt chain renders the egocentric view plus candidate frontiers as A/B/C/D markers; the VLM emits (a) a scene caption, (b) a room-type prior, (c) an “is-worth-pursuing” Yes/No gate, and (d) a softmax over the frontier markers (§3.3). 4. Planning. Frontier extraction (connected-component boundaries on the shared BEV), convex-blended scoring, and a Fast-Marching-Method (FMM) planner [ 24 ] produce a discrete action in the Habitat action space [25]. The same code path runs at every agent: only the shared BEV and BVM act as implicit communication.

3.3 VLM-as-judge frontier ranking and convex-blended UCB 

Let Ft be the set of frontier cells at step t extracted from the connected-component boundary of the explored area on the shared BEV. We rank Ft by fusing spatial geometry with VLM common sense .Up to four salient frontiers are rendered onto the egocentric image as labeled markers {A, B, C, D }

and submitted, alongside the current goal token, to a VLM-as-judge prompt; the VLM returns a softmax pVLM (f ) ∈ [0 , 1] over the markers. In parallel, each frontier inherits the BVM statistics of its anchor cell and yields a Bayesian Upper-Confidence-Bound score 

UCB( f ) = μ(f ) + β pσ2(f ), (2) where β > 0 controls the exploration weight; the resulting score inflates the value of cells that are believed relevant on average or have not been sufficiently observed. The two signals are combined through a convex blend 

s(f ) = (1 − w) pVLM (f ) + w ]UCB( f ), w = 0 .50 , (3) where ]UCB is min-max normalized over Ft. Eq. (3) treats the VLM as a high-noise oracle on 

plausibility and the BVM as a low-noise oracle on coverage ; neither fully overrules the other. An explicit is-worth gate, a Yes/No VLM prompt with threshold Perception PR( f ) ≥ 0.50 , vetoes candidates the VLM considers structurally implausible (e.g. a refrigerator frontier inside an already-explored bathroom). 43.4 Decentralized swarm coordination

## 4 Experimental Setup 

Benchmark and simulator. We evaluate on the full GOAT-B ENCH [1] val unseen split: 360 

photorealistic HM3D episodes [ 2 ], 5–10 heterogeneous subtasks per episode ( 2,669 subtasks in the primary evaluation), 36 goal categories, and three goal modalities (object name, RGB image, free-form language description) interleaved within a single episode. The maximum subtask bud-get is 500 steps; the success radius is 1 m from any instance of the goal. We use Habitat-Lab and Habitat-Sim [ 25 , 26 ] (tag challenge-2022 ) with the Stretch embodiment ( 1.41 m height, 

0.17 m radius, 1.31 m camera height) and allow sliding=False . Each agent has a forward-facing 360 ×640 RGB-D sensor at 42 ◦ HFOV plus GPS/compass; the action space is the discrete 

{MOVE FORWARD , TURN LEFT , TURN RIGHT , STOP }.

Metrics. We report standard GOAT-B ENCH metrics: Subtask SR (STOP within 1 m of any goal instance in ≤ 500 steps), SPL (geodesic-weighted), SoftSPL , DTG (mean distance-to-goal at STOP ), and episode-perfect Episode SR . We additionally split failures into false stops (deuc > 1 m ) and 

budget exhaustion (no STOP within 500 steps); the latter is critical for the perception ablation of Section 5.3. 

Configurations. Unless stated otherwise, AnyGoal runs with N = 2 homogeneous ground agents, SAM3 perception, SpaceOM-3B VLM, and CLIP ViT-B/32 as image matcher. Architectural hyper-parameters are fixed at the values introduced in Section 3: w = 0 .50 , ∆ = 25 steps, 1.5× commitment hysteresis, 100 px separation threshold with 0.6 penalty, and “is-worth” threshold 0.50 ; the UCB coefficient β and remaining implementation constants follow the released codebase. 1 In ablations, GroundedSAM uses tuned thresholds (box 0.55 , text 0.40 ) and YOLO-World uses confidence thresh-old 0.30 (coarse-grid search, appendix). 

Hyperparameter rationale and single-run protocol. The map resolution ( 0.05 m /cell , 480 ×480 

grid) lets the 1 m success radius span 20 cells, matching the fine end of the 5–10 cm range in VLFM and Modular GOAT; the convex weight w = 0 .50 , hysteresis 1.5×, and 100 px separation penalty are zero-shot defaults whose provenance is tabulated in Appendix A.1. A full val unseen run takes 2.2–6.0 days on a single RTX 4090, so every ablation cell is a single full-split rollout under fixed VLM temperatures and deterministic simulator seeds; a two-seed evaluation of the headline configuration agrees to within 0.2 pp on Subtask SR ( 52 .4 % ± 0.2 pp, Appendix D.5).

## 5 Results and Evaluation 

5.1 State-of-the-art performance in the strict physical regime 

Across the 360-episode headline run, successful subtasks resolve in 83 steps on average (17 % of the 500-step budget), while failing subtasks consume 161 steps on average; the median subtask length is 52 steps. This gap confirms that failures are dominated by premature STOP calls rather than incomplete exploration, consistent with the 80 .2 % false-stop rate in §5.3. 

Within the strict physical regime that mirrors deployable robot hardware (discrete 0.25 m 

forward steps, 42 ◦ HFOV matching the HelloRobot Stretch, no continuous teleportation), AnyGoal with N = 2 achieves 52.4 % Subtask SR at 12.7 % SPL on GOAT-B ENCH val unseen (Table 1, top block), outperforming the official Modular GOAT baseline by +27.5 percentage points on Subtask SR, a +110 % relative gain, while also exceeding SenseAct-NN Skill Chain by +22 .9 pp and the strongest prior zero-shot single-agent system, TANGO [ 14 ], by +20 .3 pp. Even the single-agent configuration alone reaches 41.9 % SR, exceeding every strict-regime prior system by +9 .8 to 

+17 .0 pp: the architectural gain therefore originates from the Gaussian BVM, the convex-blended UCB ranking, and the “is-worth” verification gate rather than from raw agent count. The lower block of Table 1 lists 3D-Mem [ 5 ] under a relaxed continuous regime in which the simulator’s action space and sensor footprint are altered; we discuss why those metrics are not on the same scale as the strict-regime numbers in §6. 

5.2 Multi-agent scaling: N = 1 → 2 → 3

Holding the AnyGoal architecture fixed and varying only the number of agents traces a monotonic scaling curve (Fig. 2): Subtask SR rises 41 .9 → 52 .4 → 55 .6 % for N = 1 , 2, 3. The first step (+10 .5 pp) is the largest and the second ( +3 .2 pp) is smaller, consistent with diminishing returns as frontier supply saturates. The N = 3 gain is concentrated in description and image-goal modalities (Appendix D.1); object-name navigation is already near-saturated at N = 2 because open-vocabulary detectors fire reliably on named objects within two agents’ combined field of view. The architectural lesson is independent of agent count: even at N = 1 , AnyGoal exceeds Modu-lar GOAT, SenseAct-NN Skill Chain, and TANGO by +9 .8 to +17 .0 pp (Table 1), so the Gaussian BVM, UCB blend, and “is-worth” gate drive most of the gap to prior art before any multi-agent contri-bution. 5.3 Perception bottleneck ablation (the 4-way test) 

We run the full GOAT-B ENCH val unseen pipeline four times under identical hyperparameters, vary-ing only the perception backbone (Table 2). SAM3 +CLIP achieves the highest Subtask SR ( 52 .4 % ); swapping CLIP for DINOv2 trades 1.7 pp of SR for marginal SPL gain, GroundedSAM costs 7.6 pp, and YOLO-World +MobileSAM costs 10 .4 pp. Two-proportion z-tests (Appendix D.4) confirm a recall-tier transition: SAM3-class detectors are significantly stronger than the GroundedSAM/YOLO-World tier ( p < 10 −5), while within-tier gaps are not significant at α = 0 .05 .The deeper finding (right-hand columns of Table 2; Fig. 3): as detector recall increases, the failure composition flips, from 42 .3 % budget exhaustion under the cautious YOLO-World +MobileSAM (the detector never returns a valid mask in 500 steps) to 80 .2 % false stops under SAM3 +CLIP (the agent rarely fails to find a candidate but fails to verify it). This is, to our knowledge, the first controlled, like-for-like demonstration that the bottleneck of zero-shot VLM navigation has shifted: for closed-set systems [ 1] the limiter is detector recall; for SAM3-class open-vocabulary systems it is goal verification at the stopping decision , making the 80 .2 % false-stop share the primary open problem (§6). 76 Discussion 

Interpreting SPL across simulation regimes. The GOAT-B ENCH literature has forked into two operating regimes. The strict physical regime ( 1; Modular GOAT, SenseAct-NN, TANGO [ 14 ], AnyGoal) fixes discrete 0.25 m steps, 30 ◦ turns, a 42 ◦ HFOV (HelloRobot Stretch), and 

allow sliding=False . A relaxed continuous regime used by 3D-Mem [ 5] and DORAEMON [ 27 ]substitutes Habitat’s oracle pathfinder (collapsing the SPL trajectory denominator from thousands of steps to a handful of waypoints) and a 120 ◦ HFOV camera; we read 3D-Mem’s 44 .7 % SPL as a measurement under a different evaluation contract rather than a comparable upper bound on AnyGoal’s 12 .7 % . Inside the strict regime, AnyGoal’s SPL exceeds SenseAct-NN ( 12 .7 vs 11 .3); the marginal deficits to Modular GOAT and TANGO ( −4.5 and −3.8 pp) are the direct cost of the 

+27 .5 and +20 .3 pp Subtask SR advantages under a uniform BVM prior that physically tests more frontiers per subtask. 

## 7 Limitations and Future Work 

Transparent-object floor (perception assumption). Twelve goal categories ( glass , shower glass ,

handrail , . . . ) score 0 % SR across all four detector backbones because the GoalProjector cannot anchor a back-projection on transparent, thin, or specular surfaces; a DistGate proj dist=None 

edge case further accepts STOP when no depth is available (one-line patch and a monocular-depth fusion remedy in Appendix E). 

Odometry and centralized-simulator assumption. BVM fusion assumes consistent BEV align-ment across agents; the real-world deployment relies on Vicon ground truth to isolate this variable, and GPS-denied operation at scale would require onboard SLAM or UWB-anchored relative localiza-tion. The simulator further shares the BVM as an in-process tensor; multi-machine deployment would require explicit map synchronization with non-trivial bandwidth ( ∼ 18 MB per full BEV broadcast). 

Single-run evaluation. Due to the compute constraints noted in §4, each ablation cell is a single rollout. A second-seed re-run of the headline configuration is provided (Appendix D.5), but a multi-seeded sweep across all backbones remains future work. 

Path efficiency gap (future work). AnyGoal’s strict-regime SPL ( 12 .7 % ) trails Modular GOAT (17 .2 % ) and TANGO ( 16 .5 % ) by 3–5 pp because a uniform BVM prior obliges the agent to physically test more frontiers per subtask. Two avenues preserve zero-shot generalization while closing this gap: pre-priming the BVM from a VLM layout caption (“ovens lie near sinks”), or distilling AnyGoal trajectories into a learned policy over BVM features while keeping the discrete physical action space. 

## 8 Conclusion 

AnyGoal is a decentralized, training-free multi-agent architecture that fuses VLM common sense with a lightweight 2D Gaussian Bayesian Value Map. On GOAT-B ENCH val unseen it reaches 52.4 % 

Subtask SR ( +27.5 pp over Modular GOAT) at N = 2 and 41.9 % at N = 1 , a new state-of-the-art in the strict physical regime; the gain is architectural rather than a function of agent count, and under SAM3 80 .2 % of failures are false stops , shifting the open problem of zero-shot navigation from

B.3 Frontier vs. history ( Stage-FN )

System prompt ( SystemPrompt.py:441--467 ), summarized: the VLM is shown a top-down semantic map with frontier candidates (black dots, uppercase letters A–D), historical observation points (green dots, lowercase letters a–d), and the robot’s pose, and asked to answer Yes (explore a new frontier) or No (revisit a historical point). B.4 Frontier ABCD selection ( Stage4-Select )

System prompt ( SystemPrompt.py:470--517 ). The VLM is given the same top-down semantic map plus the robot’s egocentric view; for each labeled frontier it estimates (i) distance, (ii) target probability under a common-sense placement prior, (iii) an efficiency score = target probability /

distance. The output is constrained to a single letter ∈ { A, B, C, D }, which the parser converts to the softmax over markers. The historical-point variant ( SystemPrompt.py:523--551 ) follows the same template with lowercase markers.

## E The Transparent-Object Floor and DistGate 

Twelve goal categories score 0 % subtask SR across all four detector backbones: glass (0/44), shower glass (0/13), book (0/45), boiler (0/12), christmas tree (0/18), footrest (0/10), hanging clothes (0/46), 

photo (0/9), and four other context-dependent categories ( parapet , handrail , stair , exercise bike ). Two failure paths converge on these categories. 

Depth unreliability. Transparent ( glass , shower glass ), reflective, and thin-flat ( photo , calendar )goals are detected in RGB by the VLM but the depth channel is absent or noisy, so the GoalProjector cannot back-project the detection to a stable BEV cell. Without a depth anchor the BVM is never updated for these categories, leaving them invisible to the frontier planner. 15 DistGate edge case. The current STOP gate ( main.py:3239--3253 ) rejects STOP when the goal projection distance exceeds max stop dist = 3 .0 m , but logs and accepts STOP when no projection is available at all:   

> elif pdist is None: logging.info(f"[DistGate] Agent {ai }:STOP requested but " f"no proj dist available (GoalProjector didn’t detect).") # action[ai] remains 0 (STOP) --- NOT overridden

A one-line patch ( action[ai] = 1 ) converts the no-projection branch from a logging hole into a hard STOP rejection, requiring depth evidence at every stop:    

> elif pdist is None: logging.info(f"[DistGate] Agent {ai }:STOP requested but " f"no proj dist available --- STOP rejected.") action[ai] = 1 # override: no depth, no stop

Categories expected to recover under the patch are those that fail through under-projected detections rather than truly invisible RGB signals (e.g. photo , where the GroundedSAM run logs 9 false stops at 4.3 m average euclidean distance to goal). Glass and shower-glass categories, which fail through absent RGB detections, remain inaccessible to this patch and motivate the monocular-depth fusion line of future work in §7.

## G Real-World Heterogeneous Deployment 

Platform. The real-world swarm comprises a Unitree Go1 quadruped (single-agent validation) with the same software stack ported to ROS2 in the hetspace-ros2 repository. Per-robot compute 16 is an NVIDIA Jetson AGX Orin; the sensor is an Intel RealSense D455 RGB-D camera streamed at 424 ×240@15 fps with GPU-accelerated GLSL pipeline ( realsense-ros patched to disable a depth rotation filter that produced artefacts at the D455 mounting orientation). Ground-truth pose is supplied by a Vicon motion-capture rig at 192 .168 .50 .74 .

Mapping. The local occupancy/value map is 12 ×12 m at 0.05 m /cell ( 240 ×240 grid, world origin 

[−6.0, −6.0] ), updated at 2 Hz over depth-downsampled inputs (effective 212 × 120 projection). Log-odds occupancy uses ℓocc = 0 .7, ℓfree = −0.2, clipped to [−3.0, 3.5] with a publish threshold of 

1.5 (mapper params.yaml:15--40 ). 

Onboard perception. The heavy SAM3+SpaceOM cloud stack is replaced by afully onboard combination of YOLO-World (yolov8s-worldv2.pt ) with MobileSAM (mobile sam.pt ); YOLO confidence threshold 0.4, IoU threshold 0.5, minimum mask area 100 px 

(mapper params.yaml:29--31, 49--50 ). 

Navigation. The deployed FMM planner uses a 5-cell step ( ∼0.25 m , matching the simulator), 

0.32 m robot radius for the Go1 ( 290 mm half-width plus 30 mm margin), and a 1.0 m stop dis-tance identical to the simulator success radius. Each episode is capped at 300 discrete actions or 

600 s , whichever comes first. The VLM perception gate threshold is set to 0.60 on the real robot (nav params.yaml:41 ) versus 0.50 in simulation; the higher threshold reduces false stops on the noisier onboard detector. The VLM server runs remotely at 192 .168 .50 .185 : 8008 with a 20 s HTTP timeout ( nav params.yaml:8 ).
